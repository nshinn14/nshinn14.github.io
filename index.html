Introduction¶

In this tutorial, you will apply data science to explore correlations that exist within global terrorism data, using the Global Terrorism Database maintained by the START consortium. We will first visualize the global data in various ways to form a better intuition on possible correlations that may exist. Using compiled features such as date and location, we will hypothesize what related factors may play a role in determining the likelihood of a terrorist attack and cross reference data outside the original dataset to test our hypotheses.

IV. Insight and Conclusion¶

Due to a lot of last minute pivoting due to inability to access the data we were searching for we were unable to commit as much time to the project as we had hoped. Below, I will steps I planned to take moving forward:

Given a completely random selection of dates the distribution of terror attacks should be uniform across all days of the year. Seeing that there were distinct spikes in the frequency of attacks in the USA from day-to-day, I would have hypothesized that they were associated with specific events.

With more time, I would cross reference the dates with religious holidays and run statistics comparing the probability of the attacks falling on the holiday of a given religious group based on the data compared to the probability of it falling on the holiday given a random distribution.

There are several reasons why a specific religious groups may have a statistically significant portion of attacks falling on an observed holiday. One possibility is that a radicalist group within that community is a frequent source of terrorist activities. Driven by religious motivations, the observed holiday could have a spiritual significance inciting an attack seen as poetic in nature. Another possibility is that the religious group is more commonly the victim of terrorist activity. Radicalist groups may have incentive to attack on specific holidays during which people of this community are gathered together in celebration or observation. An intended message may also be the driving factor of the correlation in this case as well.

It's important to keep in mind that each day holds the sum of data from 47 years (1970-2016). From one perspective, 47 samples may be too few to draw conclusions from. Much of the variation in distribution may be explained by the low number of samples. From another perspective it may be too long of a period on which to analyze this kind of data. The data is drawn over the course of several decades, during which ideological movements may rise and fall out of fashion. To be more effective, the data may need to be further stratified using known features such as country or region and the associated demographic features of people in that region.
